\chapter*{Executive Summary}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Executive Summary}
\markboth{Executive Summary}{Executive Summary}

This is the single, consolidated account of \textbf{Vitrine}: the agentic pipeline that turns a hand-held
walkthrough video of a museum or heritage exhibit into a structured, object-resolved 3D scene. It draws
the whole project into one document, across its four versions. The original bid (\code{main.tex}) set the
remit; the v2 architecture (\code{main\_v2.tex}) introduced the dual splat-and-mesh representation; the v3
report (\code{main\_v3.tex}) delivered the operational layer and the modern model stack; and
this v4 account adds the end-to-end validation performed in June 2026. The three prior reports remain as
historical snapshots; this one supersedes none of them and contradicts none of them.

\textbf{Headline.} The pipeline is no longer only designed. A single walkthrough now travels the full path
on real data: LichtFeld training of a Gaussian-splat field, SAM3 concept segmentation into named objects,
a depth-aware multi-view projection that isolates each object into its own gaussian subset and ranks them
by curatorial significance, per-object meshing, and two USD products, a native LichtFeld splat USD and a
textured composed scene graph with preview renders. Running the pipeline surfaced and fixed eight concrete
defects that had blocked the path, each found by execution rather than inspection.

\begin{keyfinding}
Vitrine's central promise, an \emph{object-resolved} scene rather than a monolithic splat, is now a working
capability. It was validated on a real 80-frame indoor scene that resolved into five named objects
(sculptures, furniture, walls, floor, ceiling), each isolated into its own gaussian subset, with the key
items (sculptures, furniture) correctly ranked above the structural surfaces.
\end{keyfinding}

\textbf{Honest boundary.} This account keeps an explicit \validated{} / \wip{} / \designed{} taxonomy and
holds the line. Validation ran on a reused 80-frame reconstruction (\code{milo\_run}), not on a fresh
Google-Drive capture driven through ingest and COLMAP. The per-object meshes came from the
always-available \code{gsplat}-to-TSDF backend, not from the SOTA single-image hull generators
(TRELLIS.2 / Hunyuan3D-2.1), whose ComfyUI custom-node dependencies are staged but not built. Generative
recovery of unseen object faces (FLUX.2) and the local gemma-4 vision judge are staged, not yet wired into
the loop. Mask quality is first-pass: SAM3's per-frame detection was sparse, and the projection is
multi-view-consistent but not yet depth-tested, so object subsets are coherent but over-inclusive. Each of
these sits below as \wip{} or \designed{}, never as done.

\textbf{Structure.} Part~I records the origin and mandate (the Salford pilot and its brief). Part~II sets
out the architecture and how it reached its current shape across four versions, then the eight-stage
pipeline, the operational layer, and the SOTA model stack. Part~III reports the June 2026 validation, the
defects it exposed, and the quality-engineering and decision-record status. Part~IV covers impact, the
Horizon Europe 2027 trajectory, and the roadmap. Appendix~A carries a reduced copy of the A0 conference
poster.

\chapter{A Salford Knowledge-Exchange Pilot}

\section{Programme, team and remit}
Vitrine began as a University of Salford \emph{AI Knowledge-Exchange Community of Practice} pilot, led from
the MediaCity campus and delivered with DreamLab AI Consulting Ltd. The award is a light-touch
\pounds10{,}000 AI KE CoP pilot grant (Worktribe project 5043317). Its purpose is to test a route, on
existing digital assets, from flat 2D media to 3D objects that can live and be re-staged inside a games
engine.

The programme team is cross-disciplinary, pairing University staff with an external AI partner:

DreamLab AI Consulting Ltd is a residential training facility and deep-tech laboratory in the Eskdale
Valley, Cumbria. It works on agentic and immersive systems with a network of technical specialists and an
active collaboration with the Global Agentics Foundation, and recently built a mixed-reality agentic
collaboration platform for knowledge work between people and agents. The pilot draws on that practice
directly: six days of DreamLab consultation, inclusive of credits for the AI tools the pipeline runs, plus
backfill time for the University technical staff, with a 24~GB GPU sourced through SAMCT DevLab and 2D / 3D
cameras through the SAMCT stores.

\section{Preservation gap}
The University holds a growing catalogue of immersive, experiential, time-based arts that happen in
real time through public-facing events: \textbf{Nested Cinema} (Vera's Not Alone), \textbf{We Invented the
Weekend}, and \textbf{Lightwaves}. There is, today, no coherent or efficient pipeline to preserve or
re-stage these site-based experiences once the event is over. Heritage of this kind is fragile, often
immovable, and under-documented. Flat photography and sparse photogrammetry lose material appearance,
view-dependent light, and the spatial relationships between objects, which are exactly the qualities that
make an immersive space worth keeping.

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.96\textwidth]{preservation_workflow.pdf}
\caption{Preservation gap the pilot addresses: site-based immersive events have no efficient route into a
durable, re-stageable 3D record. Vitrine inserts an agentic capture-to-scene pipeline between the event
and the games engine.}\label{fig:preservation}
\end{figure}

\section{What the pilot set out to test}
The bid framed the aim plainly. The wording below is the brief's own.

\begin{bidvoice}
An efficient, automated AI agentic workflow to transcribe from video files and other 2D media formats to
3D objects for the emulation and preservation of assets in games engines.
\end{bidvoice}

The target environment is the University's nascent virtual MetaCampus in Unreal Engine, which already
carries the MediaCity ground floor, New Adelphi, the Old Fire Station, Energy House~2 and GMIOT. The pilot
tests whether a games engine can serve as an accessible, lower-resolution preservation space, fed by an
agentic pipeline rather than by hand modelling.

\section{From one video, four products}
That remit maps onto a concrete deliverable. From one walkthrough video, Vitrine produces four
co-registered products:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item a photoreal \textbf{Gaussian-splat field} of the whole space, the high-fidelity record;
  \item a set of \textbf{named, individually-addressable objects}, each isolated from the field and placed
        at its measured pose, the curatorially-useful decomposition;
  \item a textured \textbf{polygonal environment} mesh and per-object meshes, the games-engine-ready
        geometry; and
  \item a single \textbf{USD scene graph} binding them together with source-video provenance, importable
        straight into Unreal or any USD-aware engine.
\end{enumerate}

The work pairs with, and never forks, the upstream \textbf{LichtFeld Studio} reconstruction engine.
Vitrine is the agentic orchestration, segmentation, decomposition and assembly layer on top of it. A
\emph{vitrine} is a museum display case, which is the shape of the output: a structured case of named,
inspectable objects rather than one undifferentiated cloud.

\part{Architecture and Evolution}

\chapter{Four Versions, One Trajectory}

The project has moved through four versions in a single direction: from proving the medium, to structuring
the output, to making the system operable, to running it end to end on real data. Figure~\ref{fig:timeline}
places the four against the capability each one added.

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{timeline.pdf}
\caption{Project trajectory across four versions. Each version reused the prior contracts and added one
capability layer rather than redesigning the system.}\label{fig:timeline}
\end{figure}

\section{v1 pilot: proving the medium}
The v1.0 pilot established that 3D Gaussian Splatting is a viable preservation medium: a consumer
walkthrough can become a navigable, photoreal radiance field at capture resolution. It left an open
tension. Splat fidelity and mesh editability looked mutually exclusive, and the splat was monolithic, with
no per-object structure a curator could address.

\section{v2: dual representation under one scene graph}
v2.0 re-platformed the system onto LichtFeld Studio v0.5.2 and resolved that tension with a
\textbf{splat-and-mesh dual representation governed by a single USD scene graph}. Mesh extraction stopped
being a lossy terminal step. The Gaussian splat and the polygonal mesh are co-registered USD variants of
the same prims, so a curator can view the scene as a photoreal splat in a browser or as a game-engine-ready
mesh hierarchy in Unreal, with object-level selection in both. v2 also set the eight-stage pipeline shape,
four pluggable mesh backends, and a per-object generative reconstruction subsystem driven through ComfyUI.

\section{v3: operational layer and SOTA stack}
v3.0 added the operational scaffolding that lets an operator run the system reproducibly, specified across
seven decision records (ADR-009 to ADR-015), forty functional requirements (PRD-v3), and an additive domain
model. Its core was built and host-validated: a single human-authored \code{exhibit.toml} manifest with
\code{env:} secret indirection; a VRAM-bounded serial model lifecycle; a Docker service mesh
(\code{v2g-net}); an agent-controlled ComfyUI client; a pinned model registry with a host
idiot-check; and a Rust/Axum web onboarding tool with verified secret containment. v3 also modernised the
model stack (ADR-012) and closed the prior plaintext-secrets regression in running code.

\section{v4: validated end to end}
v4 is this account. The headline is the June 2026 run: a single walkthrough now becomes an object-resolved,
dual-USD scene on real data, with the five-object result and the eight fixed defects reported in Chapter~6.
The architecture did not change to get there. What changed is that the designed path now executes.

\chapter{Reconstruct-Then-Decompose Pipeline}

\section{Capture methodology}
Input is one hand-held 4K walkthrough at roughly 30~fps, shot to give the reconstruction overlapping views
of every surface that matters. The capture step writes a per-image metadata sidecar (blur, exposure,
perceptual hash, pose hint, source-video reference) that becomes the lineage root for everything
downstream. A single clip is the only required input; no turntable, rig or structured scan is needed.

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.96\textwidth]{capture_vs_youtube.pdf}
\caption{Capture quality matters more than capture cost. A deliberate hand-held walkthrough gives the
overlap and coverage that reconstruction needs, where incidental footage does not.}\label{fig:capture}
\end{figure}

\section{Eight stages}
The validated pipeline is a reconstruct-then-decompose flow, orchestrated by an in-container agent over the
LichtFeld tool surface. Stages run in order, each manifest-configured, checkpointed and independently
recoverable.

\centering

      -> native USD (LichtFeld convert)  +  composed textured USD (Blender)
\end{lstlisting}

\section{Two agents, cleanly separated}\label{sec:twoagent}
A design principle, unchanged since v3, separates the in-container orchestrator from the vision agent it
calls. The orchestrator decides what to run, driving the stateless stage functions and ComfyUI as tools.
The \textbf{gemma-4-26B-A4B} model is a tool it calls for artefact judgement, not a second orchestrator.
There is no hidden state machine: orchestration is explicit tool-calls over a manifest-driven
configuration. gemma-4 is multimodal (\code{Gemma4ForConditionalGeneration} ships a SigLIP vision tower and
an \code{mmproj} projector), so one model covers both the vision and reasoning roles and halves VRAM
relative to running a separate VLM.

\section{Depth-aware object isolation (D10)}
Decomposition is the step that turns a monolithic field into a structured scene. SAM3 segments the capture
into named concepts. A depth-aware multi-view projection then votes each gaussian inside or outside an
object's masks across every registered COLMAP camera, isolating each object into its own gaussian subset.
Figure~\ref{fig:depth} shows the multi-view depth evidence the projection runs over.

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.96\textwidth]{depth_montage.png}
\caption{Depth-aware isolation evidence: per-view depth across the registered cameras feeds the
inside/outside vote that assigns each gaussian to a named object. The projection is multi-view-consistent;
per-view depth occlusion is the next quality step (\S\ref{sec:quality}).}\label{fig:depth}
\end{figure}

\chapter{Operational Architecture}

\section{One human-authored input: \texttt{exhibit.toml}}
v3 gave the pipeline a single declarative input. A TOML manifest carries the exhibit identity, an object
sub-list (each object with a stable \code{id}, a \code{sam3\_concept}, and a \code{priority}), the Drive
URL, \code{env:}-indirected secrets, a \code{[pipeline]} override block, and an \code{[oversight]} block.
The loader resolves the \code{env:} references at load time, strips resolved secrets before writing the JSON
run snapshot, and materialises the existing \code{PipelineConfig}. A missing referenced environment
variable fails by name. Objects marked \code{priority = "key"} enter the per-object hull-recovery path.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Illustrative exhibit.toml fragments (ADR-013/015).}]
[exhibit]
name        = "Egyptian Gallery — Case 12"
description = "Glass medallions and funerary objects"

[[objects]]

\section{Serial model lifecycle: bounding peak VRAM}
Stages run serially behind a \code{ModelLifecycleManager}. Each stage declares a \code{ModelSpec} (engine,
checkpoint, VRAM estimate, GPU affinity, isolation tier). The manager asserts headroom, loads the model,
yields to the stage, then unloads: soft (ComfyUI \code{/free} plus \code{cuda.empty\_cache}) by default,
hard (container stop/start) for the heavyweight FLUX.2 to Hunyuan3D transition. Peak VRAM is therefore
bounded to the largest single stage rather than the sum of all models, which is what lets the system run the
most-performant model per stage on a single GPU.

\section{\texttt{v2g-net}: a three-container service mesh}
Service endpoints resolve by DNS name on a Docker bridge, \code{v2g-net}: \code{comfyui:8188} for the graph
API, \code{comfyui:3001} for the in-container control API, and \code{agent-vlm} for the unified model. The
lifecycle manager owns container start/stop on this network. Figure~\ref{fig:mesh} shows the orchestrator
calling gemma-4 to see, submitting graphs to ComfyUI, and driving model lifecycle through the control API.

\section{Agent-controlled generative recovery (ADR-014)}
Objects seen from one side are completed by a generative sub-pipeline, then re-emplaced at their surveyed
pose as separate, inference-reconstructed objects. The orchestrator runs a verify-or-retry loop per
recovery target: plan a FLUX.2 inpaint or Hunyuan3D graph, submit it, have gemma-4 score the result against
the object identity, and choose accept, re-prompt within a bounded retry budget, or veto. Every attempt and
verdict is written to the per-video ledger and the \code{v2g:*} lineage, so the loop annotates and never
silently drops. Figures~\ref{fig:recovery} and \ref{fig:object360} show the verdict step and a recovered
hull.

\section{Vitrine Onboarding: the web setup tool (ADR-015)}
Setup runs through a frameworkless HTML/CSS/JS frontend backed by a single statically-linked Rust + Axum
binary. Forms are schema-driven: a JSON Schema is the single source of truth, and TOML round-trips with
\code{toml\_edit}, preserving comments and key order. A \code{/api/hardware} endpoint probes GPUs, RAM and
disk, and recommends the most-performant per-stage model and quant that fits the VRAM budget; an over-budget
override is flagged, not silently accepted. Secrets stay server-side: the browser never sees a credential,
and the manifest holds only \code{env:} references. The boundary is clean: setup makes the system runnable,
and the agent decides how to run it.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{SAM3 \#507.} The SAM~3.1 \code{addmm\_act} fused op casts to bfloat16 and never restores,
        crashing segmentation; patched at the source binding.
  \item \textbf{gsplat SH-degree.} The PLY loader hardcoded 45 spherical-harmonic coefficients but the
        trained field is SH degree~1; it now reads the actual degree and zero-pads.
  \item \textbf{Hunyuan3D kwargs.} A \code{turbo} keyword crashed the client; kwargs are now filtered to
        the constructor signature.
  \item \textbf{LichtFeld runtime.} The binary needs the host CUDA-13 runtime, vcpkg OpenUSD libs and
        \code{libz-ng}; resolved via \code{LD\_LIBRARY\_PATH}, with a Dockerfile fold-in to follow.
  \item \textbf{Depth-aware projection (D10).} Three sub-defects (empty-mask frames inflating the vote,
        absent SH vertex colours, and world-space alignment) were fixed to isolate objects correctly.
  \item \textbf{Native USD path.} Rewired from the never-running MCP server to the headless CLI
        \code{convert}.
  \item \textbf{Blender bake.} The glTF-import \code{world} root was selected during bake; deselected.
  \item \textbf{Blender USD export.} Blender 5.0 dropped a \code{usd\_export} keyword; kwargs are now
        filtered to the operator's RNA properties.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Known quality limits}\label{sec:quality}
The isolation mechanism works; its quality is first-pass. SAM3 detected each object in only a minority of
sampled frames, so the multi-view vote runs over few views. The projection is multi-view-consistent, and
parallax filters gross background, but it is not yet depth-tested per view, so object subsets are coherent
but over-inclusive. Tightening this is the first quality task: more sampled frames, per-view depth
occlusion, and denser SAM3 prompts. It is a quality task on a working path, distinct from the functional
milestone above.

\section{Security: FINDING-006 closed}
The prior audit's FINDING-006 (secrets exposed as plaintext environment variables) is closed in the running
code. \code{HF\_TOKEN} and the Google service-account credential resolve through \code{env:} indirection and
never serialise into the JSON run snapshot; \code{PipelineConfig.save()} strips secret-named fields before
write. The onboarding tool holds a pasted token in a \code{0600} \code{.secrets.env}, never echoes it, and
emits only \code{env:} references into the manifest, verified end to end. Remaining hardening, specified but
not yet implemented: browser OAuth with PKCE and CSRF \code{state}, and a URL allowlist on the server-side
proxy for the Google Drive grant.

\section{Verification spine}
PRD-v3 defines 27 acceptance gates, of which 20 are blocking. Unit surfaces pass today (31/31 across
\code{model\_lifecycle} and \code{comfyui\_control}), and the manifest loader, preflight and SOTA
idiot-check are exercised on the reference host. The full acceptance harness is not yet built: it needs a
curated golden test scene (a short walkthrough plus a ground-truth object manifest and artifact
annotations), which is the prerequisite for the two precision gates (key-item and VLM-artifact, both
$\geq 90\%$) and for calibrating the \code{min\_object\_gaussians} threshold. Authoring that fixture and
automating the gates against it is the single most valuable validation task remaining.

\chapter{Impact, Partners and Next Steps}

\section{Impact for Salford}
For the University, the pilot delivers a proof-of-principle toolset and a business case for using a games
engine as an accessible preservation space, plus cross-disciplinary CPD and knowledge exchange for academic
and technical staff. It also models a contractual relationship with a private-sector partner that respects
IP for civic value: open source and Creative Commons where appropriate, shared IP where not. A technical
report, alongside access to the core code and tools, gives the University an evidence base it owns.

\begin{recommendation}
Vitrine has crossed the line from designed to demonstrated: a real capture now becomes an object-resolved,
dual-USD scene. The next increment of value is quality and SOTA hulls on the same validated path, then a
fresh-capture run, not new architecture. The honest gaps in Part~III are the backlog, in priority order.
\end{recommendation}

\section{Horizon Europe 2027 and partners}
The pilot deliberately aligns with a planned application to the EU Commission Horizon Europe 2027 Culture,
Creativity and Inclusive Society programme (Cluster~2), specifically HORIZON-CL2-2027-01-HERITAGE-08:
safeguarding and transmission of intangible cultural heritage. The proof-of-principle toolset and the
working relationship with a UK CCI AI partner are the evidence base for that bid. Current and proposed
international partners include Rhizome (USA) and Onassis ONX (USA and Greece), with the Waag FutureLab
(Netherlands) and V2 (Netherlands) proposed, alongside the ARA (Archives and Records Association) and the
ICA (International Council on Archives). The 2003 ICH Convention, adopted by 148 countries, frames
intangible heritage as living heritage that continually transforms, which is exactly what a structured,
re-stageable capture is meant to keep alive.

\section{Roadmap}
The work that turns the validated milestone into a production capability, in rough dependency order:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Quality.} Densify SAM3 sampling and add per-view depth occlusion to the D10 projection.
  \item \textbf{SOTA hulls.} Build the TRELLIS.2 / Hunyuan3D-2.1 ComfyUI custom-node dependencies, replacing
        the gsplat-TSDF fallback for key objects.
  \item \textbf{Recovery.} Wire the FLUX.2 unseen-face inpaint loop and stand up the local gemma-4 VLM as
        the verify-or-retry judge.
  \item \textbf{Metadata.} Populate the \code{v2g:*} per-object USD customData, run the native-export parity
        probe, and persist per-object pose.
  \item \textbf{Fresh capture.} Exercise the Drive\,$\rightarrow$\,ingest\,$\rightarrow$\,COLMAP path end to
        end on a new capture with credentials.
  \item \textbf{Assurance.} Author the golden test fixture, automate the acceptance gates, and fold the
        \code{libz-ng} runtime lib and host-staged weights into the container image.
  \item \textbf{Scale.} Generalise the single-exhibit manifest to multi-room collections in the MetaCampus,
        add WebXR delivery of the ksplat + USD scene, and run a curated public capture programme across the
        University's heritage and teaching collections.
\end{enumerate}
