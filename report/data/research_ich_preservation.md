## 1. UNESCO 2003 Convention for the Safeguarding of the Intangible Cultural Heritage

### Summary

The UNESCO Convention for the Safeguarding of the Intangible Cultural Heritage, adopted on 17 October 2003, defines ICH as "the practices, representations, expressions, knowledge, and skills that communities, groups, and individuals recognize as part of their cultural heritage." ICH is transmitted from generation to generation, constantly recreated in response to environment and history. The Convention identifies five broad domains: oral traditions, performing arts, social practices/rituals/festive events, knowledge concerning nature and the universe, and traditional craftsmanship.

### Digital Preservation Challenges

1. **Embodied and Tacit Knowledge**: Unlike tangible heritage manifested through physical objects, ICH expressions are defined through tacit reliances and embodied practices that are bodily communicated, enacted, socially transmitted, and constantly evolving. Few methods have fully supported the recording, representing, and reviving of the living nature of ICH.

2. **Intellectual Property and Community Rights**: Digital tools raise questions of local cultural property rights. While the Convention outlines conditions regarding copyright of photos and videos attached to ICH elements, legal ambiguities have enabled online uploading of such attachments without adequate protection, necessitating more stringent copyright regulations.

3. **Technological Obsolescence**: Born-digital and digitised cultural materials face rapid obsolescence of formats, software, and hardware. UNESCO's 2003 Charter on the Preservation of the Digital Heritage acknowledges the "disappearing digital heritage" problem.

4. **Context vs. Artefact**: ICH is inherently performative and contextual. A video recording of a ritual captures only a fraction of its multisensory, social, and spiritual dimensions. The challenge is preserving not just the documentation but the experiential and participatory qualities.

5. **Community Consent and Control**: The Convention emphasises community participation in identification and safeguarding. Digital preservation must navigate questions of who controls access, representation, and reuse of digitised ICH.

## 3. Preserving Immersive and Experiential Art

### 3.1 Rhizome

**Founded**: 1996 (affiliated with the New Museum, New York)
**Mission**: Champions born-digital art and culture through commissions, exhibitions, scholarship, and digital preservation.

**Key Programmes**:
- **ArtBase**: Founded 1999; the largest collection of historical and contemporary born-digital artworks in the world.
- **Net Art Anthology** (2016--2019): Two-year online exhibition restaging 100 key artworks from the history of net art.
- **Preservation Services**: Led by Dragan Espenschied; specialises in conservation of net art, born-digital artworks, CD-ROMs, artist-made games, and multimedia publications. Uses emulation to ensure older digital projects run as originally intended inside newer technical environments.
- **Open-source tools**: Creates free, open-source software tools to decentralise web archiving and software preservation practices (e.g., Webrecorder/Conifer).

**Core Challenge**: Net art is highly vulnerable -- after only a few years, as internet standards and software evolve, online art projects may behave differently or stop working entirely. Rhizome documents how projects work, what they require technically, and uses emulation rather than migration as the primary preservation strategy.

### 3.2 Onassis ONX

**Organisation**: Onassis Foundation (Greece)
**Locations**: Studios in New York City (Tribeca, 6,000 sq ft) and Athens
**Mission**: Global platform for art and advanced technologies; supports genre-defying new media and immersive projects.

**Key Activities**:
- Provides artists and teams with technical, curatorial, and professional services
- Capacity-building programmes, incubation, applied research, acceleration services, seed funding, exhibition opportunities, fellowships
- Supports work at the frontiers of AI, immersive media, spatial computing, gaming, and emergent time-based media
- **TECHNE: Homecoming** (2026): Inaugural group exhibition at new Tribeca space gathering immersive works by seven artists

**Relevance to Preservation**: ONX represents the institutional investment model for commissioning and presenting immersive art, but raises questions about long-term preservation of works created with rapidly evolving technologies (game engines, VR hardware, AI systems).

## 4. The Experience Economy and Out-of-Home Entertainment Trends

### Foundational Theory

B. Joseph Pine II and James H. Gilmore coined the term "experience economy" in a 1998 Harvard Business Review article, describing the next economic stage following the agrarian, industrial, and service economies. Their framework identifies four realms of experience along two axes (participation: passive/active; connection: absorption/immersion):

1. **Entertainment**: Passive absorption (e.g., watching a concert)
2. **Education**: Active absorption (e.g., attending a workshop)
3. **Esthetic**: Passive immersion (e.g., visiting a museum gallery)
4. **Escapism**: Active immersion (e.g., VR experiences, interactive theatre)

Pine and Gilmore (2020 update) note that experiences now function as commodities developed to create deep involvement and easy sharing, with modern consumers drawn to experiential consumption that creates emotional connections and personal development.

## 5. Game Engines (Unreal Engine) as Preservation Spaces

### Existing Projects

#### TRIC Digital Homecoming (South Korea)
The Technology Research Institute for Culture & Heritage (TRIC) builds digital replicas of cultural assets using Epic Games' ecosystem, digitally preserving global heritage across cultural, intangible, natural, and future heritage categories. TRIC adopted Unreal Engine to produce real-time content while preserving quality, recreating traditional 2D artwork such as "Seven Jeweled Mountain" as spatial, dynamic 3D content.

#### Balzi Rossi Museum Virtual Reality (Italy)
Evolved from UE4 to UE5, leveraging Unreal Engine 5's advanced features (Nanite, Lumen) to create a more realistic and visually immersive virtual representation of the archaeological museum, now exhibited on-site.

#### Uruk-VR Archaeological Project (Iraq)
The Uruk-3D-model is visualised in Unreal Engine 5, becoming the digital twin "Uruk-VR" -- an end-to-end digital twin creation of the archaeological landscape in Uruk-Warka.

#### Yangxin Cloth Paste ICH Platform (China)
Digital models of handmade intangible cultural heritage are imported into Unreal Engine 5.1 for a real-time interactive virtual platform supporting dynamic transmission of ICH practices.

#### Musical Heritage Preservation
Unreal Engine is employed for virtual museum environments for music heritage, leveraging its robust 3D rendering and physics simulation for high-quality virtual exhibition halls.

### Why Game Engines for Heritage

- Real-time rendering at high fidelity (Nanite virtualised geometry, Lumen global illumination)
- Cross-platform deployment (PC, mobile, VR/AR headsets, web via Pixel Streaming)
- Interactive and participatory experiences align with ICH's living, performative nature
- Large developer ecosystem and extensive documentation
- Unreal Engine is recognised as one of the principal development engines for immersive VR in cultural heritage

## 6. 3D Gaussian Splatting for Cultural Heritage

### Foundational Work

3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) was introduced by Kerbl et al. at SIGGRAPH 2023. The method represents scenes using anisotropic 3D Gaussians initialised from sparse Structure-from-Motion point clouds, with interleaved optimisation and density control, and a fast visibility-aware rendering algorithm achieving real-time framerates (>=30 fps).

### Heritage-Specific Projects and Papers

#### Damaged Statue Visualisation (2025)
Web-based 3D visualisation of damaged statues using Gaussian splatting with integrated web interface, published in npj Heritage Science.

#### Enhanced Documentation of Cultural Artifacts (ISPRS 2025)
Comparative study of laser scanning, photogrammetry, and 3DGS for cultural artifact documentation. Found 3DGS exhibited significantly faster data processing and minimal hardware requirements, well-suited for rapid visualisation on web or mobile platforms.

#### Beyond Digital Twins: Hakka Mansion, Hong Kong (SIGGRAPH 2024)
Integrates 3DGS with real-time game engines for crossmedia cultural heritage representations, using a historic Hakka mansion as case study. Published in SIGGRAPH 2024 Conference Talks.

#### Immersive Heritage through Gaussian Splatting (Frontiers 2025)
Explores 3DGS as a new visual aesthetic for reality capture in heritage contexts. Published in Frontiers in Computer Science.

#### UAV-Based Modern Architectural Heritage (AGILE 2025)
Uses UAV-based data acquisition with 3DGS for modern architectural heritage, achieving high realism with smooth lighting transitions while preserving facade textures, structural patterns, and decorative designs.

#### AI-Assisted Temple Arts Preservation (2025)
Application of 3DGS to preservation and reconstruction of temple arts at Nakhon Si Thammarat National Museum, Thailand.

#### CULTURE3D Dataset (ICCV 2025)
Large-scale and diverse dataset of cultural landmarks for 3DGS and NeRF research, published at ICCV 2025.

#### Geoheritage: Minerals to Virtual Tours (2025)
Multi-scale study digitising landscape-scale outcrops (UAVs), architectural-scale museum interiors (smartphones), and specimen-level minerals, demonstrating 3DGS captures view-dependent phenomena.

### Comparative Advantages for Heritage

- **Speed**: Significantly faster data processing than photogrammetry
- **Hardware**: Minimal computational requirements
- **Web deployment**: Well-suited for browser-based and mobile viewers
- **View-dependent effects**: Captures reflections, translucency, and lighting changes
- **Challenges**: Image alignment errors in dynamic capture environments; background interference from scene-level reconstruction

## 7. NeRF/Radiance Field Approaches to Heritage Preservation

### Overview

Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF), introduced by Mildenhall et al. (2020), use neural networks to optimise a volumetric scene function from a set of input views, enabling photorealistic novel view synthesis. NeRF excels at rendering reflective and translucent surfaces that challenge conventional photogrammetry.

### Heritage Applications

#### ISPRS Review (2023)
Comprehensive review of NeRF and potential applications to digital cultural heritage, covering sculptures, archaeological remains, sites, and paintings. Identifies particular strength with metallic, translucent, and transparent surfaces.

#### Multi-Scale 3D Modelling (ISPRS 2024)
Demonstrates NeRF for multi-scale 3D modelling of cultural heritage artifacts, from individual objects to entire sites.

#### Katolička Porta, Novi Sad (2024)
Case study applying NeRF combined with drone-acquired high-resolution images to produce detailed digital replicas of this cultural heritage site in Serbia.

#### Frequency and Tensorial NeRF (npj Heritage Science, 2025)
Combines frequency encoding and tensorial decomposition for digital preservation of ancient buildings, published in npj Heritage Science.

#### Submerged Roman Archaeological Site
NeRF methods tested on data from a SIFET benchmark campaign at a submerged Roman archaeological site, demonstrating capability in challenging underwater conditions.

### Comparative Assessment: NeRF vs. Photogrammetry

Under conditions of reduced input data or lower image resolution, NeRF outperforms photogrammetry in preserving completeness and material description for the same set of input images. NeRF is recommended for scenarios requiring extensive area mapping with limited images, particularly in emergency/disaster documentation situations. NeRF performs well with homogeneous textures, variable lighting conditions, reflective surfaces, and fine details.

### NeRF vs. 3DGS for Heritage

| Feature | NeRF | 3D Gaussian Splatting |
|---------|------|----------------------|
| Rendering speed | Slow (ray marching) | Real-time (>=30 fps) |
| Training time | Hours | Minutes to hours |
| Novel view quality | High | High (comparable) |
| Reflective surfaces | Strong | Moderate |
| Web deployment | Difficult | Easier (point-based) |
| Editing/manipulation | Difficult | Easier (explicit geometry) |
| Hardware requirements | GPU-intensive | Lower |

## 9. UK Arts Council England Creative People and Places Programme

### Overview

Launched in 2012, Creative People and Places (CPP) focuses on parts of England where engagement in creativity and culture is significantly below the national average (bottom 33% of local authorities according to the Active Lives Survey). The programme brings together local people with professional artists, curators, producers, and performers to change lives through creativity and culture.

### Key Data

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Current investment | GBP 38.3 million |
| Number of places funded | 38 |
| Engagements (2012--2019) | 7.4 million |
| Funding source | National Lottery |
| Current funding round | 2026--2029 National Portfolio |

### Programme Aims

1. Engage more people from eligible places in a wide range of arts and cultural experiences
2. Empower communities to lead and shape local cultural provision
3. Ensure excellence and relevance in engagement processes and creative offerings
4. Take an Action Research approach to community engagement in arts, creativity, and culture

### Delivery Model

CPP funds consortium partners in eligible local authority areas to empower local people to decide what kind of creative activity they want to experience in their place. This consortium model -- bringing together local authorities, arts organisations, community groups, and sometimes universities -- is distinctive and relevant to ICH preservation as it centres community agency.

### Relevance to ICH Preservation

- Community-led decision-making mirrors UNESCO Convention principles of community participation
- Action Research methodology aligns with iterative, participatory approaches to heritage safeguarding
- Focus on places with low cultural engagement addresses access and representation gaps
- Potential model for how digital preservation projects could embed community co-design

## 10. Open Source Licensing Models for Cultural Heritage Technology

### Creative Commons for Cultural Heritage

Creative Commons (CC) licenses and public domain tools provide a simple, standardised mechanism for cultural heritage institutions (museums, libraries, archives) to make heritage materials and metadata openly available. Key principles:

- Digital reproductions of public domain materials must stay in the public domain -- no new copyright applies to the digitised version
- CC0 (Public Domain Dedication) is recommended for metadata and data
- CC BY (Attribution) and CC BY-SA (Attribution-ShareAlike) are common for original digital content
- CC licenses facilitate free access, use, and reuse of heritage materials

**Recommended Licenses for Cultural Heritage Content** (Creative Commons 2025 guidance):
- **CC0**: For metadata, datasets, and digital reproductions of public domain works
- **CC BY 4.0**: For original interpretive content, educational materials
- **CC BY-SA 4.0**: For collaborative/community-generated content where share-alike is desired

### Open Source Software for Heritage

#### Archivematica
- **License**: GNU Affero General Public License (AGPL 3.0)
- **Purpose**: Open-source digital preservation system
- **Features**: Conforms to OAIS reference model, handles ingestion, processing, storage, and access of digital objects

#### Arches Project
- **Developed by**: Getty Conservation Institute and World Monuments Fund
- **License**: GNU AGPL 3.0
- **Purpose**: Open-source data management platform for cultural heritage inventories
- **Features**: Semantic data structuring, geospatial capabilities, responsive UI, CIDOC-CRM compliant
- **Adoption**: Used worldwide by heritage management agencies, universities, municipalities
- **Notable deployment**: EAMENA project (Oxford University) recording archaeological sites across 20 MENA countries

#### Omeka
- **License**: GPL
- **Purpose**: Free, open-source CMS for online digital collections
- **Features**: Publishing and exhibiting cultural heritage objects, extensible with themes and plugins

#### IIIF (International Image Interoperability Framework)
- **Type**: Open standard (not software, but an interoperability framework)
- **Formed**: 2015 IIIF Consortium (11 founding institutions)
- **Purpose**: Universal standards for describing and delivering images over the web
- **Components**: Image API, Presentation API, Content Search API, Authentication API
- **Adoption**: Backed by consortium of leading cultural institutions worldwide

#### Other Relevant Open Standards/Tools
- **Webrecorder/Conifer** (by Rhizome): Web archiving tools, open source
- **CIDOC-CRM**: Ontology standard for cultural heritage documentation (ISO 21127)
- **Dublin Core**: Metadata standard used across heritage institutions

### Licensing Considerations for Heritage Technology Projects

| Use Case | Recommended License | Rationale |
|----------|-------------------|-----------|
| Software/code | AGPL 3.0 or Apache 2.0 | Ensures derivative works remain open; Apache for permissive |
| 3D models/scans | CC BY 4.0 or CC0 | Enables reuse; CC0 for public domain originals |
| Research data | CC0 | Maximises scientific reuse |
| Educational content | CC BY-SA 4.0 | Attribution with share-alike for community benefit |
| Metadata | CC0 | Standard practice for interoperability |

## Cross-Cutting Themes and Synthesis

### Key Gaps Identified

1. **ICH-specific 3DGS/NeRF work is sparse**: Most radiance field heritage work focuses on tangible heritage (buildings, artifacts). Almost no published work applies these methods to intangible heritage (performance, ritual, craft practice).

2. **Preservation of immersive art itself**: While organisations like Rhizome and ONX commission and present immersive art, systematic preservation frameworks for game-engine-based, VR, and real-time rendered artworks are underdeveloped.

3. **Community participation in technical workflows**: The UNESCO Convention and CPP programme both emphasise community agency, but most 3DGS/NeRF/game engine heritage projects are researcher-led with limited community co-design.

4. **Interoperability standards for 3D heritage**: IIIF addresses 2D images effectively, but no equivalent open standard exists for 3D Gaussian splat or NeRF scene data in heritage contexts.

5. **Long-term sustainability**: Game engine versions change rapidly (UE4 to UE5 migration already required for some projects). No established protocol for ensuring real-time heritage experiences remain accessible across engine generations.

### Opportunity Space

A project combining:
- 3D Gaussian Splatting capture (rapid, low-cost, high-fidelity)
- Unreal Engine real-time rendering (interactive, cross-platform)
- Community co-design (CPP/UNESCO principles)
- Open licensing (CC/AGPL)
- ICH focus (performance, craft, ritual -- not just buildings)

...would address a significant gap in the literature and align directly with the Horizon Europe HERITAGE-08 call objectives.
